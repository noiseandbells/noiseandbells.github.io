The Tale of Brampton

Jeremiah Jebediah Johnathaniel P. Brampton III, Esq. lived on Dibble Street in Sioux Falls, South Dakota. Unbeknownst to his neighbors, Mr. Brampton's home was infested with chunky green fruit. The Bramptons had a hobby of picking and selling other peoples' fruit trees. The neighbors thought Brampton was a real estate agent for a large time investor. It was a misdiagnosis.

Brampton was working as an agent for an Incredibly Large Quince.

He entered an urban legend in South Dakota for being the harbinger of the apocalypse. The rumors around Sioux Falls ranged from being the only market for huge quinces, to the end of the world. It didn't matter. He had 15 to 20 people at his disposal at any given moment. The clippers were out and loaded on Brampton's Volvo and he was headed off to find another buyer for the giant quince.

Brampton had a friend who had access to over 200 acres of land in northern Minnesota. He had the use of a large antique farm house which was eventually destroyed in a fire. Brampton liked this guy and the house for the years he stayed there. The friendship was started when Brampton's Quince found its way to the estate of the man's family. The Quince produced fruit which was divine.

This quince would produce 10 pounds a day. For three months, the fruit was left to rot, as no one was eating the quince. On the fourth of December, the year 1989, Brampton called the family to say the deal was off. Brampton was also supposed to be there to sell the fruit. Instead, he spent the night in his van, hoping to drive back to Sioux Falls the next day to pick up his green tree. Brampton called the family at 10:00 AM, saying he was still stuck in his van. But, instead, they heard a dull thump, followed by a popping sound.

Brampton was lying in a ditch, dead. He had been struck by a stray bullet. It had broken the glass of the windshield and ricocheted off the hood, embedding itself in Brampton's head. The leaves on the tree were completely burned. The quince had to be destroyed and the branches cut down to remove the branches and check to see if the quince had enough sugar content to be good to eat. It didn't.

The farm owner, the family of Brampton, the county investigator, and the Police Chief all assumed Brampton had been murdered. He had probably been drunk and was shot in the head by someone on his property. They all claimed they had had to convince his wife that he was no more.

Someone, somewhere had had access to his dead body. It wasn't unusual in Sioux Falls. When family or friends passed away in Sioux Falls, it was the custom to open the casket, take a look at the dead body, and give him a quick $5.00, as to not upset the family. Then, the body was tossed into a crematorium.
